# Classic Scenarios of Strategic Language Interaction

A collection of well-known game scenarios in which one or more parties use language to achieve some end — persuading, deceiving, deducing, negotiating, or gaming each other through conversation.

---

## 1. Werewolf — The Bold Claim Duel (悍跳对决)

**Players:** 12 players seated in a circle.

**Setup:** 4 players are secretly Werewolves (they know each other); 4 are ordinary Villagers; 4 have special roles — the Seer (who learns one player's true identity each night), the Witch (who can save or poison one player), the Hunter (who can take someone down upon death), and the Idiot (who survives one vote). Roles are dealt face-down. No one reveals their card.

**How the game works:** The game alternates between Night (eyes closed; Werewolves silently choose a victim, Seer checks one identity, Witch may act) and Day (open discussion, then a majority vote to eliminate one player). Eliminated players reveal their card and leave. Villagers win when all Werewolves are dead. Werewolves win when they equal or outnumber the remaining good players.

**The classic scenario:** On the first Day, a Werewolf stands up and claims to be the Seer, announcing fabricated "verification results" from the night ("I checked Player 5 — they are a Werewolf"). The real Seer must then also claim Seer, presenting their genuine results. The entire table now faces two players making the same claim with contradictory information. Every other player must judge — based solely on how each speaks, the logic of their claims, their body language, and their voting behavior — which one is lying. The real Seer must sound authentic without seeming rehearsed; the fake Seer must maintain a fabricated story under cross-examination from 10 other players. This confrontation, called 悍跳 (bold claim), is the signature dramatic moment of competitive Werewolf.

## 2. Avalon — Merlin's Dilemma

**Players:** 5–10 players divided into two hidden teams: Loyal Servants of Arthur (good) and Minions of Mordred (evil). Typical ratio: 5 players → 3 good, 2 evil.

**Setup:** Roles are dealt secretly. Evil players open their eyes at night and learn each other's identities. Good players know nothing. One good player is **Merlin** — during the night phase, Merlin also opens their eyes and sees who the evil players are. One evil player is the **Assassin**.

**How the game works:** The game consists of 5 rounds (Quests). Each round, a rotating Leader proposes a team to go on the Quest. All players vote to approve or reject the team. If approved, team members secretly play Success or Fail cards. One Fail card fails the Quest. Good players must always play Success; evil players may play either. The first side to win 3 Quests wins — except: if good wins, the Assassin gets one final chance to name Merlin. If correct, evil wins instead.

**The classic scenario:** Merlin knows exactly who the evil players are, but cannot simply announce it — if Merlin is too obvious in steering the group, the Assassin will identify and kill them after the good team's victory, flipping the result. Merlin must find ways to subtly influence team proposals, cast doubt on evil players without appearing omniscient, and guide the group through indirect hints. Meanwhile, the Assassin watches every player's behavior throughout the game, looking for the one person who "knows too much." Every word Merlin speaks is calibrated between two risks: say too little and the team loses Quests; say too much and the Assassin finds you.

**Variant — Percival/Morgana:** An additional layer. Percival (good) is shown two players: one is Merlin, the other is Morgana (evil, posing as Merlin). Percival doesn't know which is which. Morgana gives "helpful" advice that subtly misdirects. Percival must figure out which advisor to trust — while the Assassin watches Percival's behavior to deduce who Percival ultimately sides with, and therefore who the real Merlin is.

## 3. Spyfall (谍影迷踪) — The Specificity Tightrope

**Players:** 3–8 players. One is the Spy; the rest are Residents.

**Setup:** All players except the Spy receive an identical card showing a shared location (e.g., "Submarine," "Hospital," "Pirate Ship"). The Spy receives a card that says only "Spy" — they do not know the location.

**How the game works:** Players take turns asking any other player a question (e.g., "How do you feel about the dress code here?"). The questioned player must answer. After any question-answer exchange, any player may call a vote to accuse someone of being the Spy (requires majority). If the Spy is caught, the Residents win — unless the Spy can correctly guess the location, in which case the Spy wins. If the Spy is not caught within a time limit (typically 8 minutes), the Spy wins. At any point, the Spy may also voluntarily reveal themselves and guess the location.

**The classic scenario:** Every answer walks a tightrope. If a Resident's answer is too specific ("The oxygen levels down here concern me"), the Spy narrows the location to something underwater. If too vague ("The environment is fine"), other Residents suspect you might be the Spy bluffing. The Spy must ask and answer questions about a place they don't know — using broad, location-neutral phrasing while secretly piecing together the location from others' answers. Experienced groups engage in an arms race of ever-more-cleverly-calibrated questions that prove knowledge to allies while revealing nothing to the adversary.

## 4. Secret Hitler — The Policy Dilemma

**Players:** 5–10 players. Players are secretly divided into Liberals (majority), Fascists (minority), and one Hitler.

**Setup:** Each player receives a secret role card (Liberal or Fascist) and a secret party membership card. Fascists know each other. Hitler knows nothing (in 7+ player games). Liberals know nothing.

**How the game works:** Each round, a rotating President nominates a Chancellor. All players vote. If approved, the President draws 3 Policy tiles from a shuffled deck (containing 11 Fascist and 6 Liberal tiles), discards 1 face-down, and passes the remaining 2 to the Chancellor, who enacts 1 and discards 1 face-down. The enacted policy is placed on the board. Liberals win by enacting 5 Liberal policies or assassinating Hitler. Fascists win by enacting 6 Fascist policies or by electing Hitler as Chancellor after 3 Fascist policies are already enacted.

**The classic scenario — "I had no choice":** A Liberal President draws 3 Fascist policies (statistically common given the 11-to-6 ratio). They must pass 2 Fascist policies to the Chancellor, who must enact a Fascist policy. Both can truthfully claim "I had no liberal option." This is indistinguishable from a Fascist President who drew 2 Fascist and 1 Liberal, discarded the Liberal, and passed 2 Fascist. The post-round interrogation ("What did you draw?" / "What were you passed?") becomes a pure credibility contest with no physical evidence. A **conflict** arises when the President claims "I gave you one Liberal and one Fascist" but the Chancellor claims "I received two Fascists" — the table knows exactly one of them is lying and must choose whom to believe.

**The Hitler sleeper dynamic:** Hitler's optimal strategy in larger games is to play as a convincing Liberal — never lying, never starting fights, building trust — so that after 3 Fascist policies are enacted, Liberals will willingly elect Hitler as Chancellor, triggering an instant Fascist victory. The most dangerous player at the table may be the quietest, most agreeable one.

## 5. 谁是卧底 (Who is the Spy) — The Semantic Proximity Trap

**Players:** 4–10+ players. One (or more) is the Spy (卧底); the rest are Civilians.

**Setup:** All Civilians receive the same secret word (e.g., "bee"). The Spy receives a different but semantically similar word (e.g., "butterfly"). No one knows which word the other group has.

**How the game works:** Each round, every player says one sentence describing their word without directly saying it. After all players have spoken, everyone votes to eliminate one player. The eliminated player is out. If the Spy survives to the final 2–3 players, the Spy wins. If the Spy is eliminated, Civilians win — unless the Spy correctly guesses the Civilians' word upon elimination, in which case the Spy wins.

**The classic scenario:** A Civilian holding "bee" must give a clue. "It flies" is too vague (applies to both bee and butterfly). "It makes honey" is too specific — the Spy now knows the Civilian word and can perfectly mimic. The Spy, holding "butterfly," must give clues that could plausibly describe either word while listening carefully to accumulating clues to reverse-engineer the Civilian word. As rounds progress, the Spy adapts their language to blend in. The endgame is tense: if cornered, the Spy attempts a final guess of the Civilian word, making every over-descriptive clue from earlier rounds a potential weapon for the Spy.

## 6. Blood on the Clocktower — The Unreliable Information Web

**Players:** 5–20+ players. A Storyteller (game master) runs the game. Players are secretly Good or Evil.

**Setup (Trouble Brewing edition):** One player is the Demon (kills one player per night). 1–2 players are Minions (evil, helping the Demon). The rest are Townsfolk and Outsiders (good). Each player has a unique role with a night ability that provides information or protection (e.g., the Empath learns how many of their living neighbors are evil; the Monk protects one player from the Demon). Crucially, the **Drunk** is a good player who *thinks* they have a Townsfolk power but actually receives false information every night. The **Poisoner** (a Minion) secretly selects one player each night to "poison" — that player's ability malfunctions and may produce false results.

**How the game works:** Night and Day phases alternate. During the Day, all players (including dead players, who get one final vote) discuss openly and may nominate players for execution. The player with the most votes is executed. Good wins by executing the Demon. Evil wins when only 2 players remain alive.

**The classic scenario:** The town gathers information from multiple Townsfolk, but some of that information is unknowingly wrong — the Drunk's results have always been false, and the Poisoner may have corrupted another player's ability. No one knows who is Drunk or Poisoned. The discussion shifts from the simpler question of Werewolf ("who is lying?") to a deeper puzzle: "who is honestly wrong?" Good players share information in good faith that may be completely fabricated by the game mechanics. Evil players exploit this by mixing real lies with the systemic noise. The town must not only identify the Demon but also figure out which data points in their collective information pool are corrupted — a fundamentally harder conversational problem than simple lie detection.

## 7. Deception: Murder in Hong Kong — The Silent Witness

**Players:** 4–12 players. One is the Forensic Scientist, one is the Murderer, one may be the Accomplice, one may be the Witness, and the rest are Investigators.

**Setup:** The Murderer secretly selects one "Means of Murder" card (e.g., Pillow, Rope, Poison) and one "Key Evidence" card (e.g., Broken Glass, Lipstick) from the cards in front of them. The Forensic Scientist sees these selections. Everyone else does not.

**How the game works:** The Forensic Scientist has a set of "Scene Tiles" with categories (e.g., "Cause of Death," "Location," "Victim's Clothing," "Weather"). For each tile, the Scientist places a bullet marker on one option (e.g., marking "Suffocation" under Cause of Death). The Scientist may not speak, gesture, or make eye contact — they communicate only through marker placement. All other players, including the Murderer, discuss openly. Investigators attempt to identify both the Means and Key Evidence in front of the Murderer. The Murderer participates as an "Investigator," subtly steering the group away from the truth. Players collectively get three rounds of tile placements and discussion. At any time, each Investigator may make one formal guess (naming a specific player's Means and Evidence).

**The classic scenario:** The Scientist places "Suffocation" under Cause of Death to hint at "Pillow." The Murderer immediately speaks up: "Suffocation — that's clearly pointing to the Rope, not the Pillow." The table debates the Scientist's intended meaning while the Scientist sits silently, unable to clarify. The Murderer must appear to be genuinely analyzing clues while redirecting interpretation. If the Murderer's redirection is too aggressive, they draw suspicion. If they stay silent, the group may converge on the truth. The Scientist's only recourse is to use subsequent tile placements to reinforce or redirect — choosing *which* tile to replace becomes itself a meta-clue.

## 8. Coup — The Bluff Escalation

**Players:** 2–6 players. Each player has 2 face-down Influence cards (drawn from a deck containing 3 copies each of Duke, Assassin, Captain, Ambassador, Contessa).

**Setup:** Each player starts with 2 coins and 2 hidden Influence cards. Cards remain face-down — no one knows anyone else's cards.

**How the game works:** On your turn, you take one action. Some actions are available to everyone (Income: take 1 coin; Foreign Aid: take 2 coins; Coup: pay 7 coins, force an opponent to lose one Influence). Other actions require claiming a specific role: Duke (take 3 coins), Assassin (pay 3 coins, assassinate someone), Captain (steal 2 coins), Ambassador (swap cards). **You may claim any role regardless of what you actually hold.** Any other player may call your bluff. If they're right (you don't have the card), you lose an Influence. If they're wrong (you do have it), they lose an Influence. Similarly, some roles can block other actions (Contessa blocks Assassination, Captain blocks stealing), and these blocks can also be challenged. Lose both Influence cards and you're eliminated.

**The classic scenario — The Assassination Showdown:** Player A claims Assassin and pays 3 coins to assassinate Player B. Player B claims Contessa to block. Neither may actually hold their claimed card. Player A must decide: challenge the Contessa claim (if B is bluffing, B loses an Influence; if B is truthful, A loses an Influence) or accept the block. Player B had to decide: attempt to block (risking a challenge) or lose an Influence. The entire exchange is a nested bluff: "I will kill you" / "No you won't" / "Are you really holding that card?" — resolved through nerve and probability assessment, since eliminated cards are revealed face-up throughout the game, constraining what bluffs remain plausible.

## 9. Cockroach Poker — The Pass-or-Call Chain

**Players:** 2–6 players. A deck of 64 cards featuring 8 types of vermin (8 cards each): cockroach, rat, stink bug, fly, bat, toad, scorpion, spider.

**Setup:** All cards are dealt evenly to all players, held as a face-down hand.

**How the game works:** On your turn, pick a card from your hand, place it face-down in front of any other player, and declare it to be any type (truthfully or not): "This is a cockroach." The receiving player must do one of two things: (A) Call it — say "True" or "False," then flip the card. If they're right, the card goes face-up in front of the giver. If they're wrong, the card goes face-up in front of them. (B) Peek at the card privately, then pass it face-down to a different player with a new declaration (which may be the same or different type). That player faces the same choice. A player who accumulates 4 face-up cards of any single type loses immediately.

**The classic scenario — The Targeted Kill:** Player C already has 3 face-up cockroaches. Player A slides a card to Player C and says "This is a cockroach." Player C is in crisis: if they call "True" and the card IS a cockroach, they instantly lose (4th cockroach). If they call "False" and the card is NOT a cockroach (say, it's a rat), they're correct — the card goes back to Player A. But if they call "False" and it IS a cockroach, they lose. They could peek and pass — but to whom, and with what declaration? Each player who has peeked and chosen to pass rather than call is itself a signal: "I saw the card and it was bad for me to call." The chain of passes creates accumulating information, but each passer may have also changed the declaration, adding layers of deception.

## 10. Sheriff of Nottingham — The Bribery Negotiation

**Players:** 3–5 players. One player each round is the Sheriff; the others are Merchants.

**Setup:** Players have hands of Goods cards: legal goods (Apples, Cheese, Bread, Chickens) and contraband (Crossbows, Silk, Pepper, Mead — worth much more).

**How the game works:** Each Merchant places 1–5 cards face-down in a "bag" (snap pouch) and declares the contents — you must declare a single type of legal good and the exact quantity (e.g., "3 Apples"), even if the bag contains contraband or mixed goods. The Sheriff then decides, for each Merchant, whether to inspect their bag or let it pass. Before deciding, Merchants may negotiate: offer bribes (coins, goods, promises), threaten, plead, or bluff. If the Sheriff inspects and finds contraband, the Merchant pays a penalty and the contraband is confiscated. If the Sheriff inspects and the Merchant was truthful, the Sheriff pays the Merchant a penalty for wrongful inspection. If the bag passes uninspected, the Merchant keeps everything.

**The classic scenario — The Triple Bluff:** A Merchant places 4 legal Apples in their bag and declares "4 Apples" — completely truthfully. They then offer the Sheriff a suspiciously large bribe of 5 gold to not inspect. The Sheriff thinks: "Why bribe if you're honest? They must have contraband." The Sheriff opens the bag, finds 4 Apples, and must pay the penalty. The truthful Merchant profited from making the truth look like a lie. Meanwhile, another Merchant stuffs their bag with contraband, declares "3 Cheese" with a poker face, and says nothing — letting their calm demeanor serve as their only defense. The game becomes a layered negotiation where bribery, counter-offers ("I'll give you 3 gold to open *their* bag instead"), and non-binding future promises ("Let me through this round and I'll let you through next round when I'm Sheriff") form an economy of trust and deception.

## 11. Diplomacy — The Stab in the Back

**Players:** 7 players, each controlling one Great Power on a map of 1901 Europe: England, France, Germany, Italy, Austria-Hungary, Russia, Turkey.

**Setup:** Each power starts with 3 units (armies/fleets) in home territories. The board shows 34 supply centers across Europe. There are no cards, no dice, no randomness of any kind.

**How the game works:** Each turn has two phases. In the **Diplomacy phase**, players negotiate freely — in any combination of private conversations, group meetings, written notes. They may make any promise, alliance, or threat. No agreement is binding or enforceable. In the **Order phase**, all players simultaneously write secret orders for each of their units (move, hold, support another unit, convoy). Orders are revealed and resolved simultaneously. A unit trying to move into a space occupied or contested by an equally supported unit bounces (fails). A unit supported by more allies succeeds. You gain a supply center by occupying it at the end of a Fall turn; you build new units equal to your supply centers. First to 18 centers wins.

**The classic scenario:** Germany and France form an alliance against England. For several turns they coordinate attacks, share intelligence, and build trust. Germany's forces are committed westward based on the plan. Then, without warning, France writes orders that attack Germany's undefended eastern flank — a "stab." France gains 2 supply centers in a single turn, crippling Germany. The betrayal was planned turns in advance; France built their position so that the stab would be devastating. Germany's only defense was to read France's intentions correctly — through the tone of their conversations, their unit positioning, and whether their promises were *too* generous. The entire game is an exercise in persuasion, trust-calibration, and the agonizing decision of when (not whether) to betray.

## 12. The Resistance — The Five-Reject Crisis

**Players:** 5–10 players secretly divided into Resistance (good majority) and Spies (evil minority). Spies know each other; Resistance players know nothing.

**Setup:** The game consists of 5 Missions. A rotating Leader proposes a team of specific players to go on each Mission.

**How the game works:** The Leader proposes a team. All players vote to approve or reject. If a majority approves, the team goes on the Mission: team members secretly play Success or Fail cards (Resistance must play Success; Spies may play either). If any Fail card appears, the Mission fails. If the team proposal is rejected, leadership passes to the next player. The first side to win 3 out of 5 Missions wins. **Critical rule:** If 5 consecutive team proposals are rejected in a single round, the Spies win automatically.

**The classic scenario:** It's the 4th Mission, score tied 2-2. Three team proposals have already been rejected. The pressure is immense — two more rejections and the Spies win by default. The current Leader (possibly a Spy) proposes a team that includes a suspected Spy. Resistance players face a dilemma: reject the team (and risk the 5th rejection auto-loss) or approve a potentially compromised team (and risk the Mission failing). Spies exploit this by strategically voting down "clean" teams early, consuming rejections, so that when they propose a team containing Spies, the Resistance is forced to approve it or lose. The entire dynamic plays out through lobbying, accusation, and voting-pattern analysis — tracking who consistently approves teams containing suspected Spies.

## 13. One Night Ultimate Werewolf — The Identity Shuffle

**Players:** 3–10 players. Roles include Werewolves, a Seer, a Robber, a Troublemaker, a Drunk, Villagers, and the Tanner.

**Setup:** Each player receives one face-down role card. Three additional cards are placed face-down in the center (unclaimed roles). Players look at their own card, then close their eyes.

**How the game works:** A single Night phase occurs. Roles activate in order: Werewolves open eyes and see each other. The Seer looks at one player's card or two center cards. The Robber swaps their card with another player's card and looks at the new card (they are now that role). The Troublemaker swaps two *other* players' cards (without looking — those players don't know they've been swapped). The Drunk swaps their own card with a center card without looking (they are now an unknown role). Then Day begins: all players discuss for a fixed time (typically 5 minutes) and then simultaneously vote. The player with the most votes is eliminated. If a Werewolf is eliminated, the Village wins. If no Werewolf is eliminated, Werewolves win. The **Tanner** wins only if *they* are eliminated (they want to die).

**The classic scenario:** You went to sleep as the Seer. You checked Player 3 and saw they were a Werewolf. But during the night, the Troublemaker may have swapped your card with someone else's — you might no longer be the Seer, and your information might now be "owned" by someone who doesn't know they have it. Or the Robber stole someone's card. When morning comes, Player 3 insists they're a Villager (because the Troublemaker swapped them and they don't know). Another player claims Seer (they might be the Robber who stole the Seer card). The Tanner acts suspiciously on purpose, hoping to get voted out. Nobody can be sure they are still the role they started as. The 5-minute discussion is a chaotic attempt to reconstruct what happened during a night of secret swaps.

## 14. Battlestar Galactica: The Board Game — The Anonymous Saboteur

**Players:** 3–6 players as crew members of the Battlestar Galactica fleeing Cylon pursuit. Some players are secretly Cylons.

**Setup:** Each player receives a Loyalty card: "You are not a Cylon" or "You are a Cylon." The ship has 4 resource dials (Fuel, Food, Morale, Population) — if any reaches zero, humanity loses. At the game's midpoint, a second round of Loyalty cards is dealt. A player who was human in the first half may become a Cylon in the second half (the "Sleeper Agent" phase).

**How the game works:** Players face a series of Crisis events requiring "Skill Checks." Each player secretly contributes cards face-down from their hand. Positive cards help pass the check; negative cards sabotage it. Cards are shuffled together before revealing, so no one knows who contributed what. After resolving the check, the group discusses: was the failure bad luck (the deck also contributes random cards) or deliberate sabotage? Additionally, players can vote to send a suspected Cylon to the Brig (preventing them from contributing to checks). A revealed Cylon is sent to the Cylon fleet and operates overtly against the humans.

**The classic scenario:** A critical Skill Check fails by 2 points. Two negative cards appeared in the pool. One could be from the random deck injection — or both could be from a Cylon saboteur. The table erupts in accusation: "Who had Engineering cards? Why didn't you contribute more?" A suspected player defends themselves: "I put in 4 positive points — the deficit must be from the deck." There is no way to verify this. Later, the Sleeper Agent phase occurs, and a player who everyone trusted in the first half now holds a Cylon loyalty card. Their previously trusted behavior is now weaponized — they have social capital to spend on sabotage. The entire game is a conversation about resource allocation, trust, and the ever-present question: "Were we unlucky, or were we betrayed?"

## 15. Dead of Winter — The Selfish Survivor

**Players:** 2–5 players as leaders of survivor factions in a zombie apocalypse colony.

**Setup:** The colony has a shared Main Objective (e.g., "Collect 8 Food samples"). Each player also receives a secret Personal Objective (e.g., "Have 3 Fuel cards in your personal supply at game end"). One player may have a Betrayer objective that directly harms the colony (e.g., "Colony morale reaches zero"). You win only if both the Main Objective is completed AND your Personal Objective is fulfilled (or, for the Betrayer, if the colony fails).

**How the game works:** Players manage survivors, search locations for supplies, fight zombies, and contribute to colony needs (food, barricades, medicine). Each round, a Crisis requires collective contribution of specific card types. Contributions are secret and shuffled. Players may also vote to Exile a suspected Betrayer — exiled players receive a new, harder objective.

**The classic scenario:** The colony desperately needs Food contributions to survive the winter. Player A has been hoarding Fuel cards for their secret Personal Objective — but from the outside, this looks identical to a Betrayer deliberately withholding resources. Player B accuses Player A of being the Betrayer. Player A cannot reveal their Personal Objective (it would give away their strategy and undermine their win condition). Player A must defend themselves while continuing to hoard — appearing selfish enough to explain their behavior but not so selfish that they get exiled. Meanwhile, the actual Betrayer (or possibly no Betrayer — the game may not have one) exploits the conflict between A and B to quietly sabotage Crisis contributions. Every conversation about resource allocation is shadowed by the question: "Are they bad, or just pursuing their own goal?"

## 16. Paranoia RPG — The Triple Agent

**Players:** 3–6 players as "Troubleshooters" serving The Computer (an insane, paranoid AI ruling an underground city), plus 1 Game Master.

**Setup:** Each player character has three layers of secrets. **Layer 1:** The Computer has assigned the group a mission (often self-contradictory or suicidal). **Layer 2:** Every character is secretly a mutant with a hidden superpower — mutants are considered traitors and terminated on discovery. **Layer 3:** Every character secretly belongs to a banned underground organization that has given them a side mission (often conflicting with The Computer's mission and with other secret societies). Each player has 6 backup clones, so "death" is frequent but not final.

**How the game works:** Players navigate the mission while managing three simultaneous deceptions. Everyone must appear loyal to The Computer while hiding their mutation and completing their secret society mission. Accusing another player of being a traitor (for any reason — mutant, secret society, incompetence) is actively encouraged by the rules. The Computer's own briefings are often classified at a level above the characters' clearance, meaning asking questions about the mission is itself a treasonous act. The primary interaction is verbal: accusations, defenses, deflection, and strategic betrayal during mission events.

**The classic scenario:** The team is ordered to deliver a package to Sector QRG. The corridor to QRG is flooded with radioactive goo. Player A's secret society wants the package destroyed. Player B's mutation (telekinesis) could solve the flooding problem but using it would reveal they're a mutant. Player C wants to delay the mission so they can complete their secret society side-task. Everyone proposes a plan, each subtly steering toward their hidden goal while accusing others of inefficiency (a low-grade treasonable offense). Player D, suspecting Player B is a mutant, files a treason report — but Player B counter-files, accusing Player D of *obstructing the mission* by wasting time on reports. The Computer, listening through omnipresent cameras, may agree with either accusation. Trust nobody. Report everyone. Complete the mission. Survive.

## 17. Fiasco RPG — The Collaborative Train Wreck

**Players:** 3–5 players. No Game Master.

**Setup:** Players choose a "Playset" — a themed collection of Relationships, Needs, Locations, and Objects (e.g., a small-town America playset might include relationships like "criminal associates," needs like "to get rich through insurance fraud," locations like "the high school parking lot"). Players collaboratively assign these elements to pairs of characters by drafting dice. Every pair of adjacent players shares a Relationship and one additional detail (a Need, Location, or Object).

**How the game works:** Play alternates in scenes. On your turn, you either choose to Establish a scene (you set it up, others decide if it goes well or badly for you) or Resolve a scene (others set it up, you decide the outcome). In Act One, players build toward their characters' goals through increasingly entangled interactions. Then the **Tilt** is introduced — two dramatic complications drawn randomly (e.g., "Guilt: someone panics" + "Failure: a stupid plan, executed to perfection"). Act Two is the fallout. At the end, dice determine each character's epilogue — most characters end badly by design.

**The classic scenario:** Using the "Main Street" playset: Player A and B are criminal associates planning an insurance scam. Player B and C are siblings — B is hiding the criminal activity from C. Player C and D are in a romantic relationship — C has a need "to get away from this town." Player D and A are connected through a location: "the abandoned warehouse" (where the insurance scam will take place). Each player's goals pull the narrative in different directions through purely conversational scenes. There are no dice rolls for persuasion or deception — success depends on how compellingly you narrate, how cleverly you set up or resolve scenes for others, and how you navigate the Tilt complications. The game mechanically rewards dramatic disaster; the language strategy is not about "winning" but about steering your character's story toward the most satisfying catastrophe.

## 18. Vampire: The Masquerade LARP — Elysium Politics

**Players:** 10–100+ players, each playing a vampire with a specific Clan, political allegiance, and personal agenda. One or more Storytellers run the game.

**Setup:** The game takes place at **Elysium** — a neutral meeting ground (e.g., an art gallery, a manor house) where **all violence is strictly forbidden** under penalty of destruction by the ruling authority (the Prince of the city). Each player has a character with: a Clan (e.g., Ventrue — aristocratic manipulators, Nosferatu — information brokers, Tremere — blood sorcerers), a political faction, personal goals (e.g., become the new Primogen of your Clan, discover who murdered your sire), and secrets (e.g., you are secretly loyal to an enemy faction, you have committed a forbidden act of diablerie).

**How the game works:** Players interact entirely through in-character conversation. There are no turns. Players move freely through the space, forming private conversations, group discussions, and political assemblies. Favors are traded. Alliances are formed and betrayed. Information is currency — the Nosferatu Clan specializes in selling secrets. Political power is gained through social maneuvering: persuading the Prince to grant you a title, blackmailing a rival with knowledge of their transgression, forming a voting bloc to oppose a policy. Physical confrontation is handled through a brief mechanical system (rock-paper-scissors or card draws), but at Elysium, even attempting violence is political suicide.

**The classic scenario:** The Prince announces that the position of Sheriff (enforcer of vampire law) is vacant. Three candidates seek the role. Each must build a coalition of supporters through private conversations — promising favors, threatening to reveal secrets, calling in old debts. Meanwhile, the Nosferatu have learned that one candidate is secretly a member of the Sabbat (an enemy sect). They will sell this information to the highest bidder — but perhaps the Sabbat agent will pay more for the Nosferatu's silence. The candidate must navigate the evening's social interactions knowing that at any moment, their secret could be exposed. Every conversation is layered: what you say, what you imply, what you're really after, and what the other person thinks you're after.

## 19. The Ugli Orange — The Hidden Interests Negotiation

**Players:** 2 players (or 2 teams), each representing a different organization.

**Setup:** A seller has 3,000 Ugli Oranges — the only available supply. Both players are told: "Your organization urgently needs Ugli Oranges. You must negotiate with the other party, who also needs them." Each player receives a confidential briefing.

**Confidential information:** Player A's organization needs the **orange rinds** to synthesize a medicine for a disease outbreak. Player B's organization needs the **orange juice/pulp** for a chemical research agent. Neither player is told what part of the orange the other needs. Each assumes the other wants the same thing they do: the whole orange.

**How the game works:** The two players negotiate face-to-face with no rules beyond a time limit (typically 30 minutes). They may share or withhold any information. There is no mediator.

**The classic scenario:** Most pairs immediately enter a distributive (zero-sum) negotiation: "I'll take 1,500 and you take 1,500" or "My need is more urgent, I should get 2,000." This feels like a fair compromise but is actually the worst possible integrative outcome — both parties get only half the supply of the part they need. If either player asks "What exactly do you need the oranges for?", the integrative solution becomes obvious: one takes all the rinds, the other takes all the pulp. Both get 100% of what they need. The exercise is the canonical demonstration of the difference between *positions* ("I need the oranges") and *interests* ("I need the rinds") — and how a single well-placed question can transform a conflict into a cooperative surplus. It is the most widely taught negotiation exercise in the world.

## 20. Sally Soprano — The Multi-Issue Trade

**Players:** 2 players. One represents Sally Soprano, a talented opera singer. The other represents the Lyric Opera company.

**Setup:** Both parties want to reach a performance contract. The negotiation involves multiple issues: base salary, number of performances, rehearsal schedule, billing (name prominence), and other terms. Each player receives a confidential scoring sheet that assigns point values to different outcomes on each issue.

**Confidential information:** The parties' priorities across issues differ. For example, Sally may value rehearsal flexibility much more than base salary, while the Opera company may care intensely about the number of performances but have flexibility on rehearsal. These relative priorities are secret.

**How the game works:** Open negotiation within a time limit. Players may share or conceal their preferences.

**The classic scenario:** If both sides anchor on salary (the most obvious issue), the negotiation becomes a tug-of-war. But if they explore each other's priorities through open-ended questions, they discover opportunities for **logrolling** — trading concessions on issues you care less about in exchange for gains on issues you care more about. Sally might accept a lower salary in exchange for generous rehearsal terms; the Opera might agree to top billing for Sally in exchange for an extra performance. The total value created exceeds what either side could achieve through pure compromise. The exercise teaches that multi-issue negotiations are not zero-sum: exploring the other side's priorities through conversation is the primary value-creation mechanism.

## 21. Hostage/Crisis Negotiation — The Behavioral Change Stairway

**Players:** 2 parties — a trained negotiator and a subject in crisis (e.g., a hostage-taker, a barricaded individual).

**Setup:** The subject has taken a hostage (or is threatening self-harm) and is in communication with the negotiator, typically by phone or through a barrier. The subject has stated demands (e.g., safe passage, money, grievance resolution). The negotiator's team may have tactical information (number of hostages, weapons, escape routes) that the subject doesn't know they possess.

**How the game works:** The FBI's Behavioral Change Stairway Model structures the negotiator's approach in five sequential stages: (1) **Active Listening** — demonstrating that you hear and understand what the subject is saying, using paraphrase, mirroring, and minimal encouragers. (2) **Empathy** — naming the subject's emotions ("It sounds like you feel betrayed by the system"). (3) **Rapport** — once the subject feels heard, a relationship of trust develops. (4) **Influence** — only now can the negotiator begin suggesting alternatives. (5) **Behavioral Change** — the subject begins to act differently (releasing hostages, surrendering).

**The classic scenario:** A former employee has barricaded himself inside a building with two hostages, demanding to speak to the CEO who fired him. The negotiator does not argue, does not promise, does not threaten. Instead: "You worked there for fifteen years. That's a long time to give to a place." (Active Listening.) "It sounds like you feel like they threw away everything you built." (Empathy.) Over hours, the subject begins talking more about his feelings than his demands. The negotiator has not conceded anything but has shifted the subject's emotional state. When the negotiator finally suggests "What if we can set up that meeting, but first we need to make sure everyone's safe?" — the subject is in a psychological state where compliance is possible. The core insight, now widely applied in business negotiation: you cannot influence behavior until you have established rapport, and you cannot establish rapport until you have demonstrated empathy.

## 22. The Prisoner's Dilemma — The Cooperation Paradox

**Players:** 2 players who cannot communicate with each other.

**Setup:** Two suspects are arrested and held in separate rooms. The police offer each the same deal independently.

**Rules:** Each player simultaneously and independently chooses to **Cooperate** (stay silent) or **Defect** (betray the other). The payoffs:
- Both Cooperate: each gets 1 year in prison (mutual reward).
- Both Defect: each gets 5 years (mutual punishment).
- One Cooperates, the other Defects: the cooperator gets 10 years (sucker's payoff), the defector goes free (temptation payoff).

**The classic scenario:** Regardless of what the other player does, each player is individually better off defecting (if the other cooperates, you go free instead of serving 1 year; if the other defects, you serve 5 years instead of 10). Both players, reasoning this way, defect — and both serve 5 years. Had they cooperated, both would have served only 1 year. Individual rationality produces collective irrationality. In the **Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma** (played repeatedly between the same players), cooperative strategies like Tit-for-Tat (cooperate first, then copy the other's last move) emerge as dominant, demonstrating how trust, reputation, and reciprocity develop over repeated interactions.

## 23. The Ultimatum Game — The Fairness Punishment

**Players:** 2 players — a Proposer and a Responder.

**Setup:** The Proposer is given $100 (or any sum) and must propose a split with the Responder.

**Rules:** The Proposer offers any division (e.g., "$70 for me, $30 for you"). The Responder either accepts (both get their shares) or rejects (both get nothing).

**The classic scenario:** Game theory predicts the Proposer should offer $1 (the minimum) and the Responder should accept (since $1 > $0). In practice, across thousands of experiments worldwide, offers below roughly 20–30% are frequently rejected, and the most common offer is around 40–50%. Responders will sacrifice their own money to punish an offer they perceive as unfair. This single experiment dismantled the model of the "purely rational, self-interested agent" and launched the field of behavioral economics. It reveals that humans have strong fairness preferences and are willing to incur costs to enforce social norms — a dynamic present in every real-world negotiation.

## 24. Chicken (Hawk-Dove) — The Brinkmanship Paradox

**Players:** 2 players.

**Setup:** Two drivers race toward each other head-on at high speed. Each must independently decide: **Swerve** (yield) or **Drive Straight** (hold course).

**Rules and payoffs:**
- Both Swerve: tie — mild embarrassment for both (moderate outcome).
- One Swerves, the other Drives Straight: the swerving driver is "chicken" (humiliated/loses), the straight driver wins (best individual outcome).
- Both Drive Straight: head-on collision (catastrophic for both — worst outcome).

**The classic scenario:** Each player wants to be the one who drives straight while the other swerves. But if both hold course, both suffer the worst outcome. The paradox: **appearing irrational becomes rational**. If you visibly remove your steering wheel and throw it out the window, the other driver *knows* you cannot swerve and must swerve themselves. Credible commitment to an irrational action forces the rational opponent to yield. This models nuclear brinkmanship (the Cuban Missile Crisis), labor strikes, political standoffs, and any negotiation where escalation threatens mutual destruction. The strategic question is always: can you convince the other side that you are committed — or crazy — enough to go all the way?

## 25. The Stag Hunt — The Coordination Gamble

**Players:** 2 players (hunters).

**Setup:** Two hunters go out together. Each independently chooses to hunt **Stag** or **Hare**.

**Rules and payoffs:**
- Both hunt Stag: they catch the stag together — large reward for both (best collective outcome).
- One hunts Stag, the other hunts Hare: the stag hunter catches nothing (the stag requires two hunters), the hare hunter catches a hare — small reward.
- Both hunt Hare: each catches a hare — small but guaranteed reward for both.

**The classic scenario:** Unlike the Prisoner's Dilemma, mutual cooperation (both hunt Stag) IS a stable equilibrium — neither player gains by unilaterally switching. But mutual defection (both hunt Hare) is also a stable equilibrium. The problem is not temptation to betray but **confidence in coordination**: hunting Stag is risky because you depend entirely on the other's commitment. If you doubt the other will choose Stag, hunting Hare is the safe choice — but if everyone plays safe, no one gets the big reward. This models adopting new technology standards, joining collective action, investing in joint ventures — any situation where the barrier to cooperation is not selfishness but trust.

## 26. The Trust Game — The Reciprocity Test

**Players:** 2 players — an Investor and a Trustee.

**Setup:** The Investor receives an endowment (e.g., $10). They may send any portion of it to the Trustee. Whatever is sent is **tripled** by the experimenter before reaching the Trustee. The Trustee then decides how much (if any) of the tripled amount to return to the Investor.

**Rules:** If the Investor sends $10, the Trustee receives $30 and can return anything from $0 to $30. If the Investor sends $0, the game ends — the Investor keeps $10, the Trustee gets nothing. There is no enforcement mechanism for returns.

**The classic scenario:** Game theory predicts the Investor should send nothing (since the Trustee has no incentive to return anything). In practice, Investors typically send 40–60% of their endowment, and Trustees return enough that Investors profit. The game operationalizes trust as a measurable economic act: sending money is trust; returning money is trustworthiness. But trust is fragile — a single betrayal (Trustee keeping everything) can destroy it, and the amount sent is exquisitely sensitive to context (anonymity, cultural norms, prior interactions). The game models every situation where one party must invest before the other delivers: advance payment, job offers, sharing secrets.

## 27. The Genius (Korean TV Show) — Minority Rule

**Players:** 12 contestants in a televised elimination game.

**Setup (Minority Rule episode):** All players vote simultaneously for one of two options (e.g., "Black" or "White"). The players who voted with the **minority** advance. The players who voted with the **majority** are in danger of elimination.

**Rules:** Before the vote, players may negotiate, form alliances, promise to vote together, lie about their intended vote, or attempt to persuade others to switch sides. Voting is simultaneous and secret. Multiple rounds may occur.

**The classic scenario:** If everyone coordinates to vote the same way, everyone is in the majority and everyone loses. So someone must break away — but who? Alliances form: "The five of us will vote Black. You six vote White. We'll be the minority and win." But within the alliance, defection is tempting: if you secretly vote the opposite way, you're guaranteed to be in the smaller group. Everyone is simultaneously trying to be in the minority while convincing others to join the majority. The negotiation before each vote is intense: real alliances, fake alliances, double-crosses, and attempts to calculate what everyone else is calculating. It is a live-action game theory experiment where the Nash equilibrium requires mixed strategies — and humans must execute them through social manipulation.

## 28. The Mole — The Hidden Saboteur

**Players:** 10–13 contestants plus one secret "Mole" planted by the producers.

**Setup:** Each episode, the group attempts a collaborative challenge (e.g., a physical task, a puzzle, a strategic exercise) to add money to a collective prize pot. One player is the **Mole** — their job is to subtly sabotage challenges without being detected. At the end of each episode, all players take a multiple-choice quiz about the Mole's identity, behavior, and characteristics. The player who scores lowest on the quiz is eliminated.

**Rules:** The Mole must sabotage, but too obviously and they'll be identified quickly. Other players must observe carefully to gather evidence for the quiz — but they also benefit from performing well on challenges (to increase the prize pot they might win). Some non-Mole players deliberately act suspiciously to mislead others' investigations.

**The classic scenario:** During a challenge, the team must cross a river by building a raft. Player A "accidentally" drops a crucial tool into the water, costing the team 10 minutes. Player B makes a wrong turn that leads the team away from the checkpoint. Player C gives incorrect instructions to the team during a communication challenge. One of them is the Mole; the others are simply clumsy or are performing badly to draw suspicion away from the real Mole. Every evening at dinner, players analyze the day's events through conversation, each privately building a theory about who the Mole is while revealing as little as possible about their own theory (since a correct theory is your competitive advantage in the quiz).

## 29. Liar Game — Minority Rule Variant

**Players:** A group of contestants playing a high-stakes game with real financial consequences (in the manga/TV series, losing means massive debt).

**Setup (Minority Rule arc):** Similar to The Genius, but with escalating stakes. Players vote in multiple rounds, and the minority survives while the majority is penalized. Between rounds, players may trade information, form alliances, and negotiate.

**Rules:** Voting is secret. Players may negotiate vote-trading agreements: "I'll vote Black if you guarantee three others will vote White." However, agreements are not enforceable — anyone can lie. Eliminated players lose real money (in the series context).

**The classic scenario:** The protagonist (Nao Kanzaki) discovers that in a game designed to reward deception, the optimal strategy is paradoxically **radical honesty and cooperation**. She proposes a scheme where all players coordinate their votes so that each person takes turns being in the minority, ensuring everyone survives equally. But this requires universal trust — and one defector can ruin the entire scheme. The dramatic tension: can a cooperative system hold when every individual has an incentive to defect, and when the system is operating among people who were selected precisely because they are skilled at deception? The Liar Game's recurring thesis: the "winning" strategy in repeated social games is often cooperation, but only if you can build and enforce trust — which itself requires strategic language.

## 30. Two Rooms and a Boom — The Identity Market

**Players:** 6–30+ players physically divided into two separate rooms.

**Setup:** Each player receives a face-down card showing their team (Red or Blue) and their role. The **Blue Team** has a hidden President. The **Red Team** has a hidden Bomber. There are also many special roles with unique win conditions. Players can be in either room regardless of team.

**Rules:** The game is played over 3 timed rounds (typically 5, 4, and 3 minutes). Each room elects a **Room Leader** who selects "hostages" — players who are traded to the other room at the end of the round. At the end of the final round, if the **Bomber is in the same room as the President**, the Red Team wins. Otherwise, Blue wins.

**How information works:** At any time, a player can approach another player and propose either a **color share** (both privately reveal only their team color) or a **card share** (both privately reveal their full card, including role). You can refuse. You can also lie about what you saw after a share (though you can't show a fake card). Room Leaders are elected by majority and can be overthrown by majority vote.

**The classic scenario:** You're the Blue President. Do you card-share with someone to build an ally network? If that person turns out to be Red, they now know *exactly* who you are and will campaign to get you traded into the Bomber's room. You could stay hidden and hope not to be randomly selected as a hostage, but then your team can't protect you. The entire game is a marketplace of trust: "I'll share with you if you share first." Blue team members who identify each other can lobby the Room Leader to keep the President safe and trade suspected Red players. Red team members try to identify the President through information gathered via card shares, then get the President traded into the Bomber's room. The lobbying of Room Leaders, the campaign speeches for new leaders, and the private card-share negotiations are the entire game — no board, no cards played, just conversation in a room under time pressure.

## 31. British Parliamentary Debate — The Ally as Rival

**Players:** 8 debaters in 4 teams of 2 (Opening Government, Closing Government, Opening Opposition, Closing Opposition), plus a panel of judges.

**Setup:** A motion is announced (e.g., "This House would abolish the death penalty"). Teams are randomly assigned to Government (supporting the motion) or Opposition (opposing it). Each speaker delivers a 7-minute speech in a set order.

**Rules:** Each team is ranked 1st through 4th by the judges. Opening teams set up the debate framework and key arguments. Closing teams must introduce a fundamentally new angle ("extension") that adds something the Opening teams did not cover. Critically: **teams on the same side of the motion (e.g., Opening Government and Closing Government) are nominally allies but are ranked against each other.**

**The classic scenario:** You are Closing Government. Opening Government has made a strong moral argument for the motion. You must support the same conclusion, but you need to introduce an argument so compelling that the judges rank you above Opening Government. If your extension merely repeats their points, you rank below them. If your extension contradicts them, you undermine your own side. The language strategy is to **reframe the debate**: "Opening Government rightly pointed out the moral argument. But the debate must go further — the *economic* case is even more decisive." You must simultaneously credit your allies (or the judges penalize you for undermining your own side) and surpass them (or the judges rank you below them). You're persuading the judges while competing with your own teammate.

## 32. Courtroom Cross-Examination — The Commitment Trap

**Players:** 2 parties — a cross-examining attorney and a witness.

**Setup:** The witness has already given direct testimony (their version of events). The attorney has access to the full case file: depositions, prior statements, documents, and physical evidence — much of which the witness may not know the attorney possesses.

**Rules:** The attorney may only ask questions (not testify). The witness must answer. The jury/judge observes. The attorney's goal is to undermine the witness's credibility or elicit admissions that support the opposing case. The witness's goal is to maintain their testimony.

**The classic scenario:** The attorney asks a series of seemingly innocuous questions that the witness answers comfortably: "You were at the intersection at 5 PM?" "Yes." "The sun was setting?" "Yes." "You were wearing your prescription glasses?" "Yes." "You saw the defendant clearly?" "Absolutely." Then the trap closes: "Your optometrist's records from last month show your prescription is significantly out of date. You told your optometrist you'd been having trouble seeing at a distance. Is that correct?" Each early answer was a **commitment** — the witness locked themselves into a position (clear visibility, wearing glasses, confident identification) that the final question demolishes. The attorney knew the trajectory from the first question; the witness did not see the trap until it was sprung. The strategic principle: build a chain of commitments before revealing the contradiction.

## 33. Keith Johnstone's Status Game — The Silent Hierarchy

**Players:** 2 performers (and optionally an audience).

**Setup:** Each performer is secretly assigned a status number from 1 (lowest) to 10 (highest). They are given a scenario (e.g., "You are meeting at a bus stop" or "One of you is a doctor, the other a patient").

**Rules:** Perform the scene. Communicate your assigned status **only through behavior** — never state it, never reference it explicitly. High-status behaviors include: stillness, direct eye contact, speaking slowly, occupying central space, not laughing at your own jokes, finishing sentences, and choosing not to respond to low-status bids. Low-status behaviors include: fidgeting, broken eye contact, self-correction, yielding space, excessive apologizing, nervous laughter, and incomplete sentences. After the scene, the audience guesses each player's number.

**The classic scenario:** Player A (assigned 8) and Player B (assigned 3) meet at a bus stop. Player A stands still, makes eye contact, speaks in short declarative sentences. Player B shuffles, glances away, over-explains. The audience reads the hierarchy clearly. The game becomes more interesting when a third instruction is added — **at some point during the scene, reverse your status**. Player A must gradually lose status (becoming uncertain, fidgety, deferential) while Player B must gradually gain it (becoming still, direct, commanding) — without any verbal acknowledgment that the shift is occurring. The exercise reveals that every human conversation contains an invisible status negotiation, and that status is communicated almost entirely through non-verbal and paraverbal cues rather than content.

## 34. Improv Hidden Objective — The Invisible Agenda

**Players:** 2–4 improvisers.

**Setup:** Each performer draws a secret card with an objective written on it (e.g., "Get the other person to sit down," "Make the other person say the word 'elephant,'" "Convince the other person to leave the room," "Get the other person to apologize to you").

**Rules:** Perform a scene using standard improv rules ("Yes, and" — accept and build on what your scene partner offers). You must achieve your secret objective within the scene while keeping the scene coherent and naturalistic. You cannot directly state your objective or break character. If the audience can tell what your secret card says, you lose style points. Other performers are simultaneously pursuing their own hidden objectives.

**The classic scenario:** Player A's card says "Make the other person open a window." Player B's card says "Get the other person to kneel down." The scene is set in an office. Player A starts complaining about the heat: "Is it just me, or is it suffocating in here?" Player B responds: "You know what would help? Some stretching — my yoga teacher says you should start with a kneeling pose." Player A says: "Yoga! Great idea. But first, can we get some air in here? This room is terrible." Each player is steering the scene toward their goal while the other resists being steered without knowing why. The exercise mirrors real-world meetings, sales conversations, and diplomatic exchanges where participants have undisclosed agendas and use conversational framing to guide the interaction toward their desired outcome.

## 35. Socratic Elenchus — The Question Trap

**Players:** 2 parties — an Interrogator (Socrates) and a Respondent (the confident expert).

**Setup:** The Respondent holds a strong opinion on a topic (e.g., "Justice is whatever the strong decide" or "Courage is standing your ground in battle"). The Interrogator's goal is to reveal internal contradictions in the Respondent's position — not by arguing, but by asking questions.

**Rules:** The Interrogator may only ask questions. The Respondent must answer honestly (they cannot dodge or refuse). Each answer becomes a commitment that the Interrogator can reference later.

**The classic scenario (from Plato's *Republic*):** Thrasymachus declares: "Justice is the advantage of the stronger." Socrates asks: "Do rulers sometimes make mistakes about what's in their interest?" "Yes." "And if subjects obey the rulers' mistaken laws, are they doing what is *actually* advantageous to the rulers, or what the rulers *think* is advantageous?" Thrasymachus is stuck — if justice is obedience to the ruler, and the ruler's command is sometimes mistaken, then justice sometimes requires acting *against* the ruler's advantage. His definition collapses. Socrates never argued; he only asked questions. Each question seemed innocuous; together, they formed a logical vise. The strategic principle: by controlling the sequence and framing of questions, the interrogator can lead the respondent into commitments that logically contradict their original position, without ever stating the counterargument directly.

## 36. A Fake Artist Goes to New York — The One-Stroke Proof

**Players:** 5–10 players. One is the Fake Artist; one is the Question Master; the rest are Real Artists.

**Setup:** The Question Master announces a category (e.g., "Animal"). Every player receives a card with the specific word (e.g., "Cat") — except the Fake Artist, who receives a card marked "X." They do not know the word.

**Rules:** Starting from the Question Master's left, each player adds **one single stroke** (line, curve, dot) to a communal drawing on a shared piece of paper, using their assigned color (so all strokes are traceable). After two full rounds (each player draws twice), players discuss and vote on who they think the Fake Artist is. If the Fake Artist is identified by majority vote, they get one final chance: if they can correctly guess the word, the Fake Artist still wins. If the Fake Artist is not identified, the Fake Artist and Question Master both win.

**The classic scenario:** The word is "Cat." The first Real Artist draws a curved line (an ear). The second draws two dots (eyes). The Fake Artist, watching carefully, deduces "some kind of animal with pointy ears" and draws a nondescript curved line. The third Real Artist wants to draw a tail but realizes this would make "Cat" too obvious to the Fake Artist. They draw a vague body line instead. After two rounds, the drawing is ambiguous enough that the Fake Artist is considering "Cat," "Fox," or "Rabbit." During discussion, one player says: "Look — Player 4's second stroke doesn't connect to anything meaningful." Player 4 (the Fake Artist) counters: "My line was the curve of the back — see how it connects with Player 2's stroke?" The group must judge whether each player's contributions demonstrate genuine knowledge of the word while accounting for the constraint that drawing *too* clearly helps the Fake Artist guess correctly even if caught.

## 37. The Resistance — Mission Sabotage and Vote Analysis

**Players:** 5–10 players, secretly divided into Resistance members (good, majority) and Spies (evil, minority). Spies know each other; Resistance members know nothing.

**Setup:** The game consists of 5 Missions. For each Mission, a Leader proposes a team of players to send. The required team size varies by mission.

**Rules:** All players vote to approve or reject the proposed team. If approved, team members secretly play a Mission card: Resistance members **must** play "Success." Spies may play "Success" or "Fail." Cards are shuffled and revealed. If any "Fail" card appears, the mission fails. If the team is rejected, the next player becomes Leader. After 5 consecutive rejections in a single round, Spies win immediately. The first side to win 3 Missions wins.

**The classic scenario — The Late-Game Vote Analysis:** It is Mission 4, tied 2–2. A player has been tracking every vote from every round. They stand up and say: "Look at the pattern. In Round 1, Players A, B, and D all approved the team that included Player C — and that mission failed. In Round 2, Players A and D again approved a team with C, but Player B rejected it. Either B learned something, or B is covering their tracks. In Round 3..." The vote history becomes a forensic record of allegiance. Spies who consistently approve teams containing other Spies create a traceable correlation. Experienced Spies vary their votes to obscure the pattern, but this means occasionally voting against their own interest — creating a different kind of tell. The entire meta-game is a conversation about voting behavior rather than role claims.

## 38. 剧本杀 (Script Murder) — Faction Script (阵营本)

**Players:** 6–8 players, each assigned a character with a detailed backstory.

**Setup:** Players are secretly divided into 2–3 factions (e.g., in a political intrigue setting: Loyalists, Rebels, and Neutrals). Each player receives a character booklet containing: their backstory, their faction allegiance, their faction's victory condition, their personal objectives, and secrets about other characters. The game is set in a specific dramatic scenario (e.g., the last banquet before a kingdom falls, a sealed train carrying diplomats from rival nations, a merchant guild meeting where one faction plans a coup).

**Rules:** The game typically lasts 4–6 hours. Players interact through in-character conversation, rotating through structured discussion phases and free-play phases. They may share information, lie, form alliances, and make deals. At key moments, the host introduces new events or revelations that change the situation. At the end, each faction's victory condition is evaluated, and individual objectives are checked.

**The classic scenario:** You are a merchant at a guild meeting. Your faction (the Reformers) wants to elect your leader as the new Guild Master. The Old Guard faction wants to maintain the current leader. The Neutrals have individual goals (one wants to broker a specific trade deal; another wants revenge on a specific character). You do not know who is in which faction. Through conversation — probing questions, shared backstory references, coded language, and trust-testing exchanges — you must identify your allies, recruit Neutrals, and outmaneuver the opposing faction. A seemingly friendly character who offers an alliance might be an Old Guard plant gathering intelligence. A hostile-seeming character might actually be your faction ally testing whether you'll reveal yourself under pressure. The entire game is a sustained conversation in character, where every piece of information is simultaneously a tool and a vulnerability.

## 39. Harborco — The Six-Party Coalition

**Players:** 6 players, each representing a different stakeholder in a harbor development project: the Federal Government, the State Government, the Local Town, an Environmental Group, a Labor Union, and a Development Company.

**Setup:** The parties must negotiate a development plan for a harbor site. Issues include: level of industrial development, environmental protections, number of jobs created, tax revenue distribution, and infrastructure investment. Each party receives a confidential scoring sheet assigning different point values to different outcomes on each issue. A final agreement requires support from at least a specified majority of parties.

**Rules:** Open negotiation over a time limit (typically 90 minutes). Parties may form coalitions, make side deals, share or conceal their scoring preferences, and propose package deals. Any combination of parties that forms a winning coalition can impose their preferred agreement.

**The classic scenario:** The Developer and the Union discover they both benefit from high industrial development (the Developer for profit, the Union for jobs). They form an alliance. The Environmental Group and the State Government both value environmental protection and form a counter-coalition. The Federal Government and the Local Town are swing votes. The Developer approaches the Town privately: "If you support our plan, we'll guarantee 200 local hires and a community fund." The Environmental Group approaches the Town simultaneously: "If you support us, we'll ensure the tourism industry thrives, which benefits your economy more than heavy industry." The Town must evaluate two competing offers, neither fully transparent about their scoring priorities. The Federal Government sits back, waiting for both coalitions to court them, extracting maximum concessions. The exercise demonstrates that in multi-party negotiations, the process (who talks to whom first, who sets the agenda, how proposals are structured) matters as much as the substance.

## 40. Texoil — The Hidden Interests Discovery

**Players:** 2 players. One is the owner of a gas station. The other is a potential buyer.

**Setup:** The buyer wants to purchase the gas station. Each player receives a confidential briefing with their reservation price (the worst deal they'd accept) and additional information.

**Confidential information:** The seller has been running the station for decades and has interests beyond money: they want their long-time employees to keep their jobs, they want the station's name preserved, and they need flexible timing for the handover due to personal reasons. The buyer has plans for the property that, if structured differently, could easily accommodate these non-monetary interests at low cost. Neither party is told what the other cares about beyond price.

**Rules:** Open face-to-face negotiation within a time limit.

**The classic scenario:** Most pairs lock into a pure price negotiation, haggling over dollars. The buyer offers low; the seller counters high; they meet somewhere in the middle or fail to agree. But if the buyer asks open-ended questions ("What matters most to you about this sale?" "Is there anything besides price that's important?"), they discover the non-monetary interests. The buyer can then offer a higher price *or* accommodate the seller's concerns at minimal cost — keeping the station name, retaining current employees, allowing a gradual handover. For the seller, a slightly lower price with all non-monetary needs met may be far more valuable than a higher price without them. The exercise teaches that the single most powerful move in negotiation is asking "Why?" — transforming a single-issue tug-of-war into a multi-dimensional problem with integrative solutions.

## 41. The Arctic Survival Exercise — The Persuasion of Expertise

**Players:** 4–8 players in a group, plus facilitator.

**Setup:** The scenario: your plane has crash-landed in the subarctic wilderness in winter. Among the wreckage, you find 15 items (e.g., a ball of steel wool, a newspaper, a compass, a lighter, a .45-caliber pistol, a chocolate bar, a canvas, a whiskey, etc.). Each player individually ranks all 15 items from most to least important for survival. Then the group must reach a consensus ranking through discussion.

**Rules:** The group must produce a single agreed-upon ranking without voting (consensus only). After the exercise, rankings are compared against an "expert solution" prepared by survival specialists. Individual scores, group scores, and the best individual score are compared.

**The classic scenario:** Player A, a former scout, is confident that the compass is the most important item and argues forcefully. Player B, who once read a survival manual, insists the steel wool and lighter are most important (they can start a fire). Player C quietly suggests the whiskey might be dangerous (promotes hypothermia) rather than helpful, but C is soft-spoken and gets talked over. The group, persuaded by A's confidence, ranks the compass first — but the expert solution ranks it near the bottom (in subarctic conditions, the priority is warmth, not navigation; you should stay near the crash site). C's ignored suggestion about the whiskey was correct. The exercise demonstrates that **confidence and status influence group decisions more than accuracy**, and that groups only outperform their best individual member when they actively solicit and weigh minority opinions — i.e., when the language dynamics of the discussion don't suppress quiet expertise.

## 42. Everyone is John — The Internal Power Struggle

**Players:** 3–6 players as "Voices" inside John's head, plus 1 Game Master.

**Setup:** John is a man in Minneapolis. Each Voice secretly writes down an **Obsession** — something absurd they want John to do (e.g., "climb to the top of the tallest building," "eat a gourmet meal at the most expensive restaurant," "set something on fire," "get into a fistfight"). Obsessions are rated by difficulty: Level 1 (easy, worth 1 point), Level 2 (moderate, 2 points), Level 3 (hard, 3 points). Each Voice also starts with a pool of Willpower points (typically 7–10).

**Rules:** At any moment, one Voice controls John's actions. When multiple Voices want control, each makes a secret Willpower bid (any amount from their pool). Highest bid wins and takes control, spending the bid. The controlling Voice narrates John's actions, steering him toward their Obsession. When a Voice completes their Obsession, they score points. The GM describes the consequences of John's actions and the world's response. If John dies, the game ends and points are tallied.

**The classic scenario:** Voice A (Obsession: "get to the top of the tallest building") takes control and walks John toward the IDS Tower. Voice B (Obsession: "eat a fancy meal") outbids A and redirects John into a high-end restaurant. Voice A outbids again from the restaurant's upper floor. Voice C (Obsession: "set something on fire") has been saving Willpower and takes control just as John reaches the observation deck — and lights a trash can on fire, triggering the fire alarm and evacuation, ruining both A's and B's plans. The conversation between Voices ("What should John do?") is the strategic layer: subtly arguing for actions that serve your Obsession without revealing what it is, timing your bids to seize control at crucial moments, and managing your finite Willpower pool.

## 43. The Baron Munchausen — The Competitive Tall Tale

**Players:** 3–8 players sitting around a table with coins (tokens).

**Setup:** Each player starts with an equal number of coins (typically 3–5). Players are all "aristocrats at a dinner party."

**Rules:** On your turn, another player challenges you: "Baron, tell us about the time you sailed to the Moon on a ship made of cheese" (or any absurd prompt). You must narrate an elaborate, first-person account of this adventure. At any point during your story, any other player may **interrupt** by pushing forward one coin and introducing a complication: "Surely, Baron, you recall that the Moon was at that time occupied by a hostile army of cheese-eating mice?" You must now either: (a) **Accept** the complication (take the coin and incorporate it into your story), or (b) **Refuse** the complication (push forward one of your OWN coins and dismiss it: "No, no, the mice had emigrated to Jupiter by then. As I was saying..."). After all stories are told, players vote for the best story (you cannot vote for yourself). Most votes wins.

**The classic scenario:** You're telling the story of your duel with Napoleon. A rival player interrupts: "But Baron, surely you remember that you had both your arms in slings from the alligator incident the week before?" If you accept (taking their coin, which is good — coins are victory points), you must now explain how you dueled Napoleon with no arms, making the story harder but richer. If you refuse (spending your own coin), you avoid the complication but lose a resource. The strategic calculation: accept complications that you can turn into entertaining narrative (gaining coins), refuse only those that would derail your story entirely. Rivals must calibrate their interruptions — too easy, and the storyteller incorporates it effortlessly; too impossible, and the storyteller just refuses. The game replaces dice with rhetoric: your "combat power" is your improvisational storytelling ability.

## 44. Battlestar Galactica — The Sleeper Agent Turn

*See Scenario 14 for full rules.*

**The specific classic moment — The Sleeper Agent Phase:** At the game's midpoint, a second set of Loyalty cards is dealt. A player who has been trustworthy, transparent, and helpful for the entire first half may receive a "You are a Cylon" card. They now have insider knowledge: they know which players are truly loyal (because they spent the first half genuinely cooperating), they have social capital and trust, and they know the exact state of all ship resources. The table faces a nightmare: someone they trusted may have turned — but who? The conversations shift from "who has been suspicious?" to "who has been *too* helpful?" — a much harder question. The Sleeper Agent must pivot from genuine cooperation to subtle sabotage without changing their behavior pattern so dramatically that the transition is detectable.

## 45. Dead of Winter — The Personal Agenda Shadow

*See Scenario 15 for full rules.*

**The specific classic moment — The Exile Vote:** The colony is failing. Food contributions have been low. The group suspects a Betrayer. Player A has been hoarding Medicine cards (for their Personal Objective: "Have 4 Medicine in your supply at game end") and has contributed nothing to the last two Food crises. Player B proposes exiling Player A. Player A cannot explain their behavior without revealing their Personal Objective (which would harm their strategy). Player A argues: "I've been searching locations for Food — I just haven't found any. Check my contribution history in other crises." The group votes. If exiled, Player A receives a new, harder objective and can no longer win with the colony — creating a possible *new* Betrayer even if none existed before. The exile mechanic means that false accusations have consequences: punishing the wrong person can literally create the threat the group feared.

## 46. 剧本杀 — The Blizzard Mountain Villa (暴风雪山庄模式)

**Players:** 5–8 players, each assigned a unique character trapped in the same location.

**Setup:** A group of characters — strangers or loosely connected — are trapped in an isolated location with no outside contact. Classic settings include: a snowbound mountain lodge, a remote island, a stalled train, a sealed building during a storm. During the first night, one character is found dead. The killer is among the remaining players.

**Rules:** Each player receives a character booklet containing: their background story, their timeline of actions during the night, their relationship to other characters, their secrets, and potentially their motive for the crime. The game unfolds in rounds. In each round, the host reveals new evidence or events, and players discuss, share (or withhold) information from their character sheets, form theories, and ultimately vote on who they believe is the killer. The game typically lasts 3–5 hours.

**The classic scenario:** Seven guests at a mountain lodge. The host is found dead in the library. Each guest has a secret reason for being at the lodge — debts owed, love affairs, inheritance disputes, professional rivalries. Each character's booklet contains fragments: "At 11 PM, I heard footsteps in the corridor. I went to the kitchen and saw [character name] washing their hands." But another character's booklet says: "At 11 PM, I was in my room and did not leave." Someone is lying — or someone's timeline is wrong. The killer must construct a verbally consistent alibi from their character booklet while subtly casting suspicion on others. Innocent characters must share enough information to prove their innocence without revealing embarrassing secrets (which, if exposed, would make them *look* guilty). The confined setting means every conversation happens in a fishbowl — there is no escape from scrutiny, and no rescue coming.

## 47. Spyfall — The Double Agent Variant (Spyfall 2)

**Players:** 3–12 players. There may be **two** Spies who do not know each other's identity.

**Setup:** Same as standard Spyfall (Scenario 3), but with two Spy cards dealt. Neither Spy knows who the other Spy is. Both Spies know they are Spies but neither knows the location.

**Rules:** Same as standard Spyfall: ask questions, answer questions, deduce or accuse. Either Spy can independently guess the location to win. If one Spy is accused, the other Spy is still in play.

**The classic scenario:** Two Spies are both struggling to figure out the location from the Residents' answers. But they are also unintentionally helping each other — when one Spy asks a vague question, the other Spy notices ("That person seems to be fishing for information... are they the other Spy or just a cautious Resident?"). The Residents face a harder challenge: with two Spies, the base rate of suspicious behavior is higher, and any accusation has a lower chance of catching the "right" Spy. Meanwhile, each Spy wonders: "If I can identify the other Spy, I can coordinate — but if I approach the wrong person, I've revealed myself to a Resident." The two Spies may independently converge on the location from different clues, creating an emergent cooperation without explicit communication.
